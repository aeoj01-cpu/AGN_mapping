## 1. You don’t reach low enough frequencies

As we said earlier:

👉 You are not measuring:

τ(f→0)\tau(f \to 0)τ(f→0)

Instead, you’re measuring:

👉 τ(fmin⁡)\tau(f_{\min})τ(fmin​), which is already **biased low or distorted**

---

## 🔴 2. The transfer function is broad

For a disk:

- response spans a range of delays
- not a single lag

👉 This causes:

- frequency-dependent filtering
- suppression of high-frequency power

So:

👉 τ(f) is **not constant**, even ideally

---

## 🔴 3. Windowing / finite light curve length

Your observation window acts like a filter:

- distorts low-frequency behaviour
- introduces bias

---

## 🔴 4. Noise + binning

At low frequencies:

- fewer independent samples
- larger uncertainties

👉 makes plateau harder to recover

The mean lag inferred from the frequency-resolved lag spectrum does not exactly match the true mean lag calculated from the transfer function. This discrepancy arises because the lowest accessible frequencies are limited by the finite duration of the light curve, preventing measurement of the true low-frequency asymptotic behaviour. As a result, the measured lag reflects a partially converged estimate rather than the intrinsic delay.
